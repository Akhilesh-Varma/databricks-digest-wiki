## Summary
Large language models have improved rapidly in many domains but remain poor at playing video games, according to NYU Game Innovation Lab director Julian Togelius. While rare exceptions exist, such as Gemini 2.5 Pro beating Pokémon Blue in 2025, these successes required custom software, took far longer than human players, and involved repetitive errors. Togelius argues that coding resembles a well-designed game with immediate, granular feedback, which explains LLM success there, whereas video games vary wildly in mechanics, input representations, and available training data. He also notes that even non-LLM systems like AlphaZero require retraining per game, suggesting general game AI remains an unsolved problem. The lack of standardized video game benchmarks further hampers LLM progress in this domain.

## Key claims
- LLMs have no reliable ability to play video games, with only rare exceptions requiring custom software and far more time than human players.
- Gemini 2.5 Pro beat Pokémon Blue in May 2025, but this is considered an exception that proves the rule.
- Julian Togelius frames coding as a 'well-behaved game' because it provides immediate, granular feedback through compilation, execution, and test results.
- AlphaZero can play both Go and chess but required retraining and reengineering for each, illustrating the lack of general game AI.
- Games with large amounts of online guides and data, such as Minecraft and Pokémon, are more accessible to AI than lesser-known titles.
- The proliferation of benchmarks is cited as a key factor in LLM improvement in coding, a dynamic that is largely absent for video games.
- There is no general game AI, and Togelius expresses doubt that one will be achieved given the diversity of game mechanics and input representations.
