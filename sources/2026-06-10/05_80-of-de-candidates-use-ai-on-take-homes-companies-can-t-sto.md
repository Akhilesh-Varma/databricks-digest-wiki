## Summary
The article argues that take-home technical assessments for data engineering roles are fundamentally broken due to widespread, undetectable AI use. Drawing on Fabric's analysis of 19,368 interviews, the author reports that roughly 80% of candidates use AI tools on take-homes, giving them a 3:1 pass-rate advantage over honest candidates. Extended take-home assignments—now often 10–20 hours of unpaid work—have created strong economic incentives to use AI, turning the situation into a prisoner's dilemma. Detection technologies claiming high accuracy are described as ineffective theater, as OS-level overlay tools like Cluely and Interview Coder bypass screen-sharing detection entirely. The author concludes that the current system penalizes rule-following candidates and that the detection arms race is effectively over.

## Key claims
- 64% of companies prohibit AI tools in technical interviews, but 35–48% of candidates use LLMs anyway.
- 61% of candidates using AI tools pass the approval threshold without detection, giving them roughly a 3:1 pass-rate advantage over honest candidates.
- Data is drawn from Fabric's analysis of 19,368 interviews conducted between July 2025 and January 2026.
- Take-home assignments have expanded from 2–3 hours to routinely 10–20 hours of unpaid work, creating strong economic incentives to use AI.
- 83% of candidates say they would use AI on take-homes if they could get away with it.
- OS-level overlay tools such as Cluely and Interview Coder render answers via DirectX and Metal, making them invisible to screen-sharing detection.
- The shift from live coding to extended take-homes was intended to reduce bias but inadvertently created an ideal environment for undetectable AI assistance.
